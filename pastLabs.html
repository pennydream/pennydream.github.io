From Biochemistry to Neuroscience and Back

As a freshman, I wanted to find a career that was moral, acceptable, rewarding, all that shit I didn't understand but kept hearing adults talk about.

I felt like science research was for me, and nothing was more broad and complex than biochemistry, so that was where I began. Starry eyed and makin' moves.

After just two semesters, I landed a summer internship at a lab in University of Pennsylvania's School of Medicine. This was intense.

Most freshman could not find labs that wanted to teach them, probably because they were idealistic shmucks. I wasn't any different, but got lucky and knew people.

So I spent the summer of 2014 commuting to and from my parent's house.

The commute alone should be on my resume. I drove about 30 minutes to a train station, took a 90 minute regional rail ride to Philly's 30th Street Station, and walked 1.3 miles to the lab.

I completed this triathalon twice a day totaling about 4 hours of commuting. I thought working fourty hours a week was rough, but this easily made it sixty.

Regardless, this lab is where I began to face the realities of research. The P.I. had been working on essencially the same protein pathway his entire life.

The guy had put so much time and effort into trying to find a pharmaceutical solution to halting part of the body's innate immute responce to foreign cells.

This would give rise to easier organ transplants, and as many people know, organs are rare and hard to get, so making every single one work out is important.

So, I was amazed at the dedication. About a dozen people worked six to seven days a week, ten to twelve hour days, every single moment, trying to figure this out.

We rant western blots, ELISAs, cell cultures, and other protocol to find out want happened in the pathway when giving mice, rats, and non-human primates certain drugs.

I got to get my hands dirty. I was quickly brought up to speed about the theory and started running ELISAs and gels to study proteins within a week or two.

I didn't realize how important this experience was at the time. Many undergrads are not given the oppurtunity to touch these tests until they prove their ability.

So, I spent the next three months learning many protocol for how to find out what kinds of proteins are in a sample, how much of each type, and how to manipulate this.

All in all, I definitely contributed, but it was all such a rush. The big picture of the projects I was on was way too big for me. Nothing seemed real or possible. The goal was too far away.

So, I started search for other feilds. I knew that most of biochemistry and pharmaceutical research was slow, the pay was ok but usually one gets a PhD. That means four to seven more years after undergrad.

It was obvious to leave, but so difficult to know where to go.

My process was not as hard headed as one would expect. I looked at other science research feilds. I found interest in introspection and learning about the workings of my own mind.

I became more and more obsessed about this as I researched psychology, but I knew the most psychology majors did not do research. Many would not get into or go to grad school.

Psych just seemed a bit more loose. Neuroscience, on the other hand, was more of a hard science. And the turn around for research was much faster!

I wanted to be able to view my work changing others in real time. I needed that concrete difference feeling that most biochemistry research lacked. So I went into neuroscience.

As I look back, biochemistry is applied within neuroscience, so whichever major I chose, I could have gone either way. But it was a perspective flip. I had more defined goals and had learned what I wanted.
